Citation:          Peter, C. D. and Weinrich, A. (2026). Speaking Politics. Unpublished paper.
Abstract Summary:  This study investigates gender differences in political communication using 127,575 speeches from the German Bundestag (1949-2021). It finds that female MPs deliver speeches with significantly less negative and more positive sentiment than male counterparts, emphasizing environmental and social policy topics, and establishes that these differences persist within topics using an instrumental variable approach based on WWII-induced regional sex ratio variation.
Key Methodology:   Instrumental variables estimation exploiting WWII-induced regional sex ratio variation as an instrument for female representation, combined with transformer-based (BERT) and bag-of-words sentiment analysis of 127,575 Bundestag speeches (1949-2021).
Research Question: Do female politicians communicate differently from their male counterparts, and does it matter?

SUMMARY
========================================================================

Is It Credible?
This paper examines whether female members of the German Bundestag communicate differently from their male counterparts, analyzing 127,575 speeches delivered between 1949--2021. Its central claim is that speeches by female MPs are "less negative, more positive, and exhibit greater sentiment polarity," and that these differences reflect "a distinct communication style" that persists even when comparing speeches on identical topics (p. 1). To address endogenous selection into politics, the authors exploit World War II--induced changes in county-level sex ratios as an instrument for female parliamentary representation, arguing that areas with larger wartime increases in female population shares developed persistent gender norms that elevated women's political participation decades later.

The credibility of the causal claims rests heavily on the validity of the instrumental variable strategy and the interpretation of the estimated effects. While the authors acknowledge that "WWII-induced changes in county-level sex ratios may affect parliamentary speech sentiment through channels other than female political representation" (p. OS5) and conduct sensitivity analyses following Conley et al. (2012) to assess robustness to potential exclusion restriction violations, they also defend the instrument's exogeneity by noting that frontline allocation was not determined by home counties (p. 5). However, the instrument likely captures broader regional differences in wartime experience---including refugee inflows and wartime displacement---that could independently shape political rhetoric through collective memory or economic disruption, not merely through the intergenerational transmission of gender norms. Moreover, while this paper draws on established empirical literature documenting this transmission mechanism (e.g., Alesina and Fuchs-Schündeln, 2007; Laudenbach et al., forthcoming), it provides no empirical validation within its own data that counties with larger sex ratio changes actually exhibit more egalitarian gender attitudes during the sample period, leaving the theoretical pathway empirically unsubstantiated within the study's specific context.

Even if the exclusion restriction holds, the two-stage least squares estimates identify a Local Average Treatment Effect for compliers---women who entered parliament specifically because their birth counties had high WWII sex ratios---rather than the average effect for all female MPs. These women may differ systematically from other female representatives, for example coming from families with stronger traditions of female labor force participation or representing specific regional contexts, which limits the external validity of claims about female MPs generally. Furthermore, the instrument operates at the county level while the treatment is individual, creating ecological inference concerns; the first stage might reflect compositional shifts in which parties win seats rather than individual gender selection per se.

This paper's assertion that gender differences reflect a "distinct communication style" distinct from topic selection faces empirical challenges. While the authors demonstrate that female MPs emphasize different policy areas (more environment and social policy, less immigration and international affairs), they explicitly note that "topic selection constitut[es] one channel through which gender affects the overall sentiment of speeches, while a distinct communication style channel operates independently" (p. OS3). They acknowledge that including session-by-topic fixed effects attenuates the sentiment coefficients substantially---by roughly 24 percent for the BERT negativity measure---indicating that topic selection constitutes a significant channel through which gender affects sentiment. This complicates the interpretation that the findings primarily reflect communication style rather than issue specialization, yet the headline results and abstract emphasize "style" without clearly quantifying the relative contributions of these mechanisms.

The economic significance claims also warrant caution. The authors acknowledge that associations between parliamentary negativity and DAX volatility or the ifo Business Climate represent "descriptive evidence" rather than causal relationships (p. 10), yet they suggest that "gender representation may influence... how political information flows to economic agents" (p. 3). This interpretation is undermined by the finding---transparently reported by the authors---that correlations between negativity and economic outcomes are driven entirely by male MPs' speeches, while female-speaker negativity shows no significant association with market volatility (Table 5, column 4; Table 6; p. 10). This suggests the economic effects operate through a composition effect (reducing the share of male speech) rather than through distinct female information production, contradicting the narrative that female communication styles provide economically relevant variation.

Additional concerns include the application of identical sentiment classifiers across seven decades of linguistic change without validation that these measures remain comparable over time, potential post-treatment bias from including party fixed effects if the instrument affects party selection, and the lack of correction for multiple hypothesis testing across numerous sentiment measures and 21 policy topics. Despite these limitations, this paper makes valuable contributions by assembling a comprehensive corpus of parliamentary speech and documenting systematic patterns in how male and female MPs discuss policy over seven decades, even if the precise causal interpretation of these differences remains uncertain.

The Bottom Line

The causal evidence that female MPs employ systematically different sentiment in parliamentary speeches is suggestive but not definitive. While the instrumental variable strategy exploiting WWII sex ratios is creative, concerns about exclusion restriction violations (war trauma, economic disruption), the interpretation of local average treatment effects as generalizable gender differences, and the conflation of topic selection with communication style substantially weaken the claim that these differences reflect distinct female communication preferences. The economic significance claims are descriptive and actually driven by male MP negativity, undermining the argument that female representation alters information flows to markets. This paper nonetheless provides useful descriptive evidence on gender differences in parliamentary discourse and topic emphasis.

POTENTIAL ISSUES
========================================================================

Exclusion restriction violation via war trauma and economic disruption: The instrumental variable strategy relies on World War II--induced changes in county-level sex ratios affecting parliamentary discourse solely through female representation. While the authors acknowledge that "WWII-induced changes in county-level sex ratios may affect parliamentary speech sentiment through channels other than female political representation" (p. OS5) and conduct sensitivity analysis following Conley et al. (2012), the instrument captures "net demographic adjustments, including refugee inflows and wartime displacement" (p. 5), raising concerns that counties with larger sex ratio changes experienced differential exposure to bombing destruction, refugee composition (Vertriebene from Eastern territories), and post-war economic disruption. These factors could independently shape political discourse through collective trauma or economic radicalization, violating the exclusion restriction. The reliance on male-only reduced forms for calibration may itself be compromised if the instrument affects male MP composition through selection effects---a limitation the authors explicitly note: "if the instrument affects the composition of male MPs... then the male-only reduced form captures both a direct effect and a selection effect" (p. OS5). The persistence of war-related confounds threatens the causal interpretation of the gender effects on sentiment.

Local average treatment effect versus generalizable claims: This paper interprets two-stage least squares estimates as applying to female MPs generally (e.g., describing findings about "the gender composition of parliament," p. 3). However, instrumental variable estimates identify the Local Average Treatment Effect for compliers---specifically, women who enter parliament because their birth county had high World War II sex ratios. These women may differ systematically from other female MPs (e.g., coming from families with stronger female labor force participation traditions or representing rural areas with persistent labor shortages). The first-stage coefficient of 0.5026 (Table 2, column 6) implies that a one-standard-deviation increase in the instrument increases female representation by only 2 percentage points, suggesting the estimates apply to a small, selected subpopulation rather than the average female MP. The generalization from complier-specific effects to the broader population of female representatives overstates the external validity of the findings.

Unvalidated intergenerational mechanism: The identification strategy assumes that World War II sex ratios affect contemporary female representation through "intergenerational transmission of gender norms" (p. 1), citing external literature on cultural persistence. While this paper draws on established empirical evidence from prior literature establishing this mechanism, it provides no empirical validation within its own data that the instrument actually affects contemporary gender norms in the sample period, nor does it demonstrate intermediate steps such as female labor force participation rates in the 1970s--1990s for these counties. Additionally, this paper does not empirically disentangle the mechanism of cultural norm transmission from economic necessity (labor shortages). Without mediation analysis showing that counties with higher $\Delta$Female\_c exhibit more egalitarian gender attitudes during the Bundestag sample period, the attribution of effects to "gender norms" remains theoretically motivated but empirically unsubstantiated within this specific study.

Ecological inference from county-level instrument to individual treatment: The instrument operates at the county level ($\Delta$Female\_c), but the treatment---being a female MP---is individual. The first stage establishes that counties with higher female population shares produce more female MPs, but this ecological correlation does not establish that individual women elected from these counties are "treated" by gender norms rather than selected through party mechanisms. The instrument might affect which parties win seats in these counties (e.g., left-leaning parties that nominate more women) rather than causing parties to nominate women at higher rates. While this paper includes party fixed effects, these control for affiliation after selection rather than addressing whether the instrument shifts party composition at the extensive margin. If the instrument works through party composition rather than individual gender selection, the estimates capture the effect of electing MPs from certain party types rather than the effect of female representation per se.

Temporal persistence without decay: Although Online Supplement OS3 (Figure OS1) demonstrates stability in the first-stage relationship across the instrument's distribution (p. OS4), the instrument measures sex ratio changes between 1939--1946 while outcomes are measured from 1949--2021---a gap of up to 72 years. The theoretical framework assumes that "altered gender norms... transmitted across generations" (p. 2) persist indefinitely without decay. However, this paper does not explicitly test whether the first-stage relationship remains stable across specific legislative periods, particularly after German reunification in 1990 and the social changes of the 1960s--70s that might have homogenized gender norms across regions. If instrument strength decayed over time, the two-stage least squares estimates represent a weighted average that may primarily reflect early-period effects (1949--1970s) rather than the full 1949--2021 sample, limiting the relevance of the findings to contemporary parliamentary discourse.

Conflation of topic selection and communication style: This paper claims that gender differences in sentiment reflect "a distinct communication style" that "persists within topic" (p. 1). While this paper explicitly notes that "topic selection constitut[es] one channel through which gender affects the overall sentiment of speeches, while a distinct communication style channel operates independently" (p. OS3), Online Supplement Table OS1 shows that including session-by-topic fixed effects attenuates the sentiment coefficients substantially (e.g., Negative BERT drops from $-0.1323$ to $-0.101$, a 24 percent reduction). The headline results in Table 2 and the abstract emphasize "communication style" without clearly quantifying the relative contribution of topic selection versus within-topic language differences. The headline sentiment effects thus conflate two distinct mechanisms: female MPs selecting less negative topics (environment/social vs. immigration/international affairs) and female MPs using less negative language within topics.

Logical inconsistency in economic significance claims: Section 6 attempts to provide an "economic anchor" by correlating parliamentary sentiment with DAX volatility and ifo Business Climate. The authors acknowledge this represents "descriptive evidence" and that the relationship tracks contemporaneous conditions rather than establishing predictive causality (p. 10). Despite this, they interpret the correlations as evidence that "gender representation may influence... how political information flows to economic agents" (p. 3). This creates a logical tension: if sentiment merely reflects contemporaneous economic conditions (reverse causality or common shocks), it cannot simultaneously demonstrate that parliamentary discourse affects economic outcomes. Furthermore, the evidence shows that associations between parliamentary negativity and economic outcomes are driven entirely by male MPs' speeches (Table 5, column 4; Table 6), while female-speaker negativity is "small and not statistically significant" (p. 10). This contradicts the framing that female communication styles provide economically relevant variation, suggesting instead that the economic effects operate through a composition effect (reducing male speech share) rather than a distinct female information production channel.

Post-treatment conditioning and collider bias: The specifications include party affiliation fixed effects to "absorb... political role that could correlate with gender" (p. 6). However, if the instrument (World War II sex ratios) affects the type of politicians who enter politics and their party selection (e.g., women from high-$\Delta$Female\_c counties selecting into Green or Social Democratic parties), then party affiliation is a post-treatment variable. Conditioning on party fixed effects may induce collider bias, blocking part of the causal pathway from historical gender norms to speech patterns that operates through party ideology. This paper does not test specifications without party fixed effects to assess sensitivity to this choice, leaving uncertainty about whether the estimated gender effects capture communication differences or ideological positioning mediated by party selection.

Linguistic non-comparability over seven decades: This paper applies identical sentiment classifiers (BERT and bag-of-words) to speeches spanning 1949--2021, assuming sentiment is measured on a comparable metric across seven decades. However, German political language underwent substantial changes during this period, including the evolution of parliamentary norms, the influence of television (beginning 1950s), changing rhetorical styles, and reunification-related discourse shifts. The BERT model was trained on German-language samples (Guhr et al., 2020), and the bag-of-words dictionary (Rauh, 2018) was developed for German political language. While session fixed effects absorb period-specific levels, they do not address period-specific measurement error in sentiment classification. If female MPs are more prevalent in later periods when language is more "modern," the sentiment differences could reflect temporal linguistic drift rather than gender effects.

Unvalidated text classification measures: This paper relies on two sentiment measures---a general-purpose BERT classifier (germansentiment) and a bag-of-words dictionary (Rauh, 2018)---and the "parlbert-topic-german" model for topic classification, but provides no validation that these measures accurately capture sentiment or topics in German parliamentary discourse across the 1949--2021 period. Political language involves irony, sarcasm, rhetorical questions, and historical linguistic shifts that may not align with general-purpose training data. The authors do not report inter-rater reliability, manual coding of a random subsample, or correlation with alternative indicators (e.g., applause rates) for this specific corpus. While Guhr et al. (2020), Rauh (2018), and Klamm et al. (2022) describe the model architectures, out-of-sample performance on the full Bundestag corpus remains unverified, leaving uncertainty about measurement error that could bias results in unknown directions.

Birthplace-constituency mismatch: The instrument is assigned at the county of birth ("we collect detailed biographical information... with particular emphasis on their places of birth," p. 4), but German MPs are elected either through direct constituency mandates (Wahlkreise) or state party lists (Landeslisten), and many represent areas different from their birthplaces. For list MPs, there is no geographic constituency link; for constituency MPs, the birthplace may differ from the represented district. This creates measurement error in treatment assignment: an MP born in a high-$\Delta$Female\_c county but representing a different area is assigned the "treatment" of gender norms from their birthplace, but their speech may be shaped by the norms of their actual constituency. This paper lacks validation that birthplace counties correlate with representation counties, threatening the first-stage relationship's validity.

Alternative mechanism: strategic adaptation to discrimination: This paper interprets gender differences in sentiment through the lens of preference differences (risk aversion, less overconfidence) citing corporate literature (p. 3). However, the authors do not consider an alternative framework: female MPs may adopt less negative, more measured language as a strategic response to institutional discrimination or anticipated interruption (citing Miller and Sutherland, 2023), rather than due to innate preferences. If women must be more "positive" to be heard or to avoid backlash, the finding reflects adaptation to bias rather than exogenous gender differences in communication style. This paper does not test between these competing theoretical frameworks, leaving the interpretation of the sentiment gap ambiguous.

Spatial correlation, spillover, and bandwidth sensitivity: This paper acknowledges that Conley standard errors with a 200 km bandwidth include "27.9 percent of the of sampled county pairs, potentially implying a non-local dependence structure and pushing the limits of the assumptions underlying Conley's estimator" (p. 7), and that "under such conservative bandwidths [50 km], the 2SLS estimates become less precise" (p. 7). This sensitivity indicates that inference relies on spatial correlation structures that may reflect unobserved regional confounders. Additionally, the instrument operates at the county level, but parliamentary discourse occurs in Berlin where MPs from different counties interact. If gender norms spill over geographically (e.g., female MPs from high-change counties influence colleagues from low-change counties), the exclusion restriction is violated because the instrument affects control units. This paper addresses spatial correlation in errors but not substantive spillover in treatment.

Multiple hypothesis testing: This paper tests multiple sentiment measures (Negative BERT, Negativity BOW, Positivity BOW, Sentiment BOW, Polarity BOW) and 21 distinct policy topics (Figure 2) without correction for multiple hypothesis testing (e.g., Bonferroni or False Discovery Rate). With 127,575 observations, individual coefficients may achieve statistical significance while reflecting spurious correlations. The lack of multiple testing correction increases the risk of false positives, particularly for the topic analysis where 21 coefficients are estimated simultaneously.

Selection into speaking opportunities: The analysis conditions on speeches actually delivered ($N=127,575$), but not all MPs speak with equal frequency. If selection into speaking opportunities correlates with gender and sentiment (e.g., female MPs speak only when assigned to less contentious topics, or male MPs dominate controversial debates), the sample is selected on the dependent variable. This paper reports matching biographical data for 91 percent of speakers but does not analyze whether the probability of speaking differs by gender or instrument intensity, nor whether the gender-sentiment relationship holds among frequent versus infrequent speakers. This threatens the external validity of the findings to the full population of parliamentary discourse.

Confounding by cohort and party ideology: Female representation in the Bundestag increased dramatically from 1949--2021, meaning female MPs are on average younger and from different political generations than male MPs. This paper does not distinguish between gender effects and cohort/generational effects, nor does it interact gender with legislative period to test for temporal trends. Additionally, while party fixed effects absorb between-party differences, female MPs are disproportionately concentrated in left-leaning parties (Greens, SPD, Left Party). If female MPs within parties are more ideologically progressive than male colleagues, the estimated gender effect may capture ideological positioning rather than gender-specific communication styles.

Omitted variables and reverse causation in economic analysis: In Table 5, the authors regress DAX volatility on speech negativity with legislative period, person, and party fixed effects, but do not control for contemporaneous economic news or events that likely drive both parliamentary negativity and market volatility. For example, during the 2008 financial crisis or the 2020 pandemic, both parliamentary discourse and DAX volatility would spike. The fixed effects structure does not absorb these time-varying common shocks. The authors acknowledge the descriptive nature of the analysis and the ambiguous temporal ordering (p. 10), noting that the relationship tracks contemporaneous conditions rather than establishing predictive causality. However, the interpretation that "gender representation may influence... how political information flows to economic agents" (p. 3) remains problematic given that the associations are driven by male-speaker negativity and may reflect reverse causation or common shocks rather than parliamentary discourse affecting markets.

Replication and technical issues: Section 6.1 (p. 9) states that "A one-standard-deviation increase in average daily speech negativity is associated with an increase in DAX volatility of approximately 0.065 percentage points... In economic terms, the estimated effect corresponds to roughly one-tenth of a standard deviation of DAX Vol Pct LSEG,d (0.83)." ========================================================================
FUTURE RESEARCH
========================================================================

Improved identification: Future work could exploit quasi-experimental variation that more cleanly isolates gender representation from wartime confounds, such as the introduction of gender quotas in German state parliaments or random assignment of committee leadership positions. These designs would avoid the exclusion restriction violations inherent in using WWII casualties, which bundle gender ratio changes with trauma and economic disruption.

Mechanism validation: Research could directly test the intergenerational transmission mechanism by collecting survey data on gender attitudes in the counties used as instruments, or by linking historical labor force participation rates in the 1970s--1990s to contemporary parliamentary representation. This would validate whether the instrument operates through cultural norms rather than economic necessity or demographic composition.

Disentangling style and substance: Future studies could employ experimental designs where MPs respond to standardized policy scenarios, or use within-speaker variation when MPs switch committees or topics, to isolate communication style from topic selection. Natural language processing techniques that detect rhetorical strategies such as question-asking, hedging, or interruptions rather than just sentiment could better capture "communication style" distinct from issue content.

Temporal validity: Research examining whether the first-stage relationship between WWII sex ratios and female representation persisted across specific legislative periods---particularly after German reunification and the social changes of the 1960s--70s---would clarify whether the IV estimates reflect early post-war patterns or contemporary dynamics. This could involve testing for structural breaks in the first-stage relationship around 1990 or 1970.

Economic consequences: To establish whether parliamentary discourse causally affects market outcomes, future work could exploit exogenous variation in speaking time allocation or plenary session schedules as instrumental variables for discourse volume and tone, or analyze high-frequency market reactions to specific speeches using event study methodologies that account for contemporaneous news flows.

Measurement validation: Manual coding of random subsamples of speeches across different decades would validate whether BERT and bag-of-words measures capture sentiment consistently over time, accounting for linguistic drift, irony, and rhetorical conventions specific to German parliamentary history.
